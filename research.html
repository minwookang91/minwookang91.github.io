Research

From biological questions to quantitative mechanisms

I combine experimental biophysics with engineering analysis to determine how physical forces emerge in cells and how those forces regulate cell function.

01
Biological questions

Cellular & Nuclear Biophysics

My current work investigates how chromatin reorganization and nuclear-envelope mechanics generate the physical conditions for nuclear rupture during NETosis. More broadly, I study how cell and nuclear mechanics influence immune responses, cancer progression, tissue repair, and scar formation.

NETosisChromatinNuclear mechanicsCancer-cell migrationFibroblasts

Representative questions

  • How does chromatin decompaction generate intranuclear pressure?
  • What determines the mechanical threshold for nuclear-envelope rupture?
  • How do chromatin dynamics and nuclear mechanics regulate cancer-cell migration?
  • How do mechanical cues alter fibroblast phenotype and tissue remodeling?
Concept Chromatin reorganization, pressure generation, and nuclear-envelope rupture
02
Investigative toolkit

Engineering & Quantitative Approaches

I develop and combine tools that translate dynamic cellular phenomena into measurable physical variables. The goal is not simply to apply engineering methods to biology, but to use quantitative measurements to discriminate between competing mechanisms.

AFMFRAPLive-cell microscopyQuantitative imagingMachine learningMechanics-based modeling

Core approaches

  • Experimental biophysics using AFM, FRAP, and force inference
  • Live-cell fluorescence microscopy of dynamic cellular processes
  • AI-enabled quantitative image analysis and single-cell phenotyping
  • Mechanics-based modeling of cellular phenomena and custom experimental systems
Concept Measurement, computation, and mechanistic inference across scales
Future direction

Mechanobiology as a bridge

My long-term goal is to establish a research program that connects nuclear biophysics, immune-cell mechanics, and quantitative engineering to uncover actionable physical principles in disease and therapy.
