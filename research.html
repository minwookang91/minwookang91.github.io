Research

From biological questions to quantitative mechanisms

I combine experimental biophysics with engineering analysis to determine how forces and material properties emerge at cellular and nuclear scales—and how they shape cell behavior.

01
Biological questions

Cellular & Nuclear Biophysics

My current work investigates the biophysical mechanisms of nuclear-envelope rupture during NETosis, with a focus on how chromatin reorganization and nuclear-envelope mechanics interact to produce rupture. More broadly, I study how changes in chromatin organization reshape nuclear mechanics and influence cellular behavior across different cell states and cell types. A major future direction is to investigate how cancer cells reprogram chromatin and nuclear mechanics to migrate through confined environments.

Representative questions

  • What determines the mechanical threshold for nuclear-envelope rupture?
  • How do cancer cells reprogram chromatin and nuclear mechanics to traverse confined environments during metastatic dissemination?

Investigative toolkit

Engineering & Quantitative Approaches

I develop and combine tools that translate dynamic cellular phenomena into measurable physical variables. The goal is not simply to apply engineering methods to biology, but to use quantitative measurements to discriminate between competing mechanisms.

Live-cell microscopyImage analysisAI-based phenotypingAFMPhysical modeling

Core approaches

  • Live-cell image analysis for quantifying cellular and nuclear dynamics
  • AI-based phenotyping of single-cell states from cellular and nuclear morphodynamics
  • Quantitative measurement of cellular and nuclear mechanics, forces, and molecular mobility using AFM, traction force microscopy, FRAP, and geometric inference
  • Mechanistic modeling and custom experimental-device development

Future direction

From chromatin dynamics to nuclear mechanics

My long-term goal is to establish a research program centered on the inside-out mechanics of chromatin: how changes in chromatin organization reshape the mechanical state of the nucleus and, in turn, cellular behavior. Building on my work on nuclear-envelope rupture during NETosis, I aim to determine whether chromatin remodeling can generate internal stresses that deform or rupture the nucleus, and how the resulting changes in nuclear mechanics influence cellular adaptation across cell types, with cancer-cell migration through confinement as a primary model.
